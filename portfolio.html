I was incredibly lucky to work at Internet Initiative Japan's Innovation Institute during my year abroad. I was in a course discussing the inner workings of the standard internet protocol stack, which just so happened to be taught by Romaine Fontugne, the leader of IIJ's Internet Health Report project. From the connection there, I was invited to work part time for a whole nine months with direct contributions the the projects performance.

The goal of Internet Health Report is to provide a better understanding about the global internet infrastructure, and how changes in one area can affect connection times elsewhere. The prime example that we usually used to explain is a sudden 50 millisecond increase in traceroute times between Melbourne and singapore in 2018. This delay directly coincided with submarine cable cut, and the delay was resolved when the cable was replaced. With this kind of information, governing bodies or internet service providers can better understand the consequences of changes to infrastructure that may have otherwise been unclear.

My work on the project involved intake, use, and reliability of data. The system ingests massive amounts of data from the RIPE network coordination centre, which has physical infrastructure in place to take traceroutes at regular intervals all around the globe. My job was to take that data, and feed reliably feed it to the different parts of Internet Health Report, like Tartiflette and Raclette. To do so, I merged their existing intake methods with a new Kafka architecture. The script and setup would download the RIPE data, publish it through the Kafka system, and that system would track which different subscribers had or had not processed certain chunks of data. That way, in the event of an outage, procesing can continue from the logged stop point. If you want to check out the specifics, the repository is here

Advance Wars Rom-Hack

My advance wars project was something I made back in the summer between high school and college. I felt like it was proabably a good idea to start taking on "big boy projects" instead of just basic school work like "checking account systems". I didn't realize at the time that by the end I would consider what I had done not particularly relevant for my skillset. Even FURTHER I didn't realize I'd change my mind back a few years later. Now that I've made that as confusing as humanly possible, I'll try to explain what i mean

The actual "coding" for the romhack didn't involve any higher level language, or really and language at all. What it did involve was a massive block of hexadecimal values. The only way to change the game was to manually change some of those individual values, based on their corresponding effect in-game. Luckily, theres a strong community at warsworldnews.com who did some of the heavy lifting on finding some of the key values. Since my main focus was creating new levels, the key values for me were the pointers that the campaign looked at to load level data. That can be shifted to point to the dummy data at the end of the file that doesn't have any affect, and can be replaced with values that represent an entirely different level.

At the time after finishing my goal of making new levels for the first chapter of the campaign, I felt a little unsatisfied, because I hadn't really created anything or used process that involved coding. What I realized upon formally learning about pointers though, is that that entire process was firsthand experience with pointer manipulation, and I know my understanding of pointers came much faster with the experience.

Lastly, an important, though rather unfortunate learning outcome was how essential good version control is. At the time, all I was using was copies of edited roms with titles like "chapter 3 done" and "current working" to keep track of changes. What ended up happening though, is that somewhere along the line I messed up one of my pointer values, and when the game tries to load that value it crashes (and makes some very scary noises...). The crucial part though, Is that I didn't notice WHEN that pointer error happened. Up to this point, I had a save file that was on the current working level to gauge progress of course, but that meant I wasn't testing every level I had finished already before moving on. (In my defense, I still had to play every level all the way through rather than just skip to see if theyre working, which is pretty time consuming...) Thus I ended up in a situation where I have several different files, each of them having SOME of the levels working and loading properly, but none of them having all. At the time, thining the project wasn't helpful, I abandoned it there. Looking back on it I of course regret that, I no longer have a copy of my hexadecimal editor bookmarks, and it would be pretty difficult to find my level locations without it, so its not exactly an easy fix.

While its kind of a mega bummer, that means I don't have much to share. On my github, I have some of those old files, so maybe I'll go back at some point. In the mean time though, I have some pictures of the finished levels, so once I figure out a good layout I'll post some.
